Gizem Filiz

I am currently 5th year Ph.D. student in Psychological & Brain Sciences at Washington University in St. Louis. I study the intersection of memory, metacognition and motivation. My research investigates how incentives shape recognition memory, specifically how value signals at encoding or retrieval influence sensitivity, confidence, and decision bias. In a complementary line of work, I examine how feedback alters learned memory biases, providing insight into how external reinforcement interacts with internal decision processes. Beyond behavioral approaches, I integrate physiological and computational methods—using pupillometry to track effort and salience in real time, and drift-diffusion modeling to characterize the cognitive mechanisms that underlie recognition judgments. Together, these projects aim to clarify how motivational and evaluative signals dynamically shape memory performance and decision-making.

Research Interests

Source Memory in Value-Directed Encoding

Investigations into recognition memory showed that value effects depend heavily on encoding strategies. When elaborative encoding was restricted, high-value items no longer held a memory advantage. Participants also often misattributed recognition strength to high-value status, indicating a heuristic process of “value-source bias.” These findings suggest that prioritization of valuable information in memory is strategy-driven rather than automatic

Pupillometry and Value-Directed Encoding

Research on value-directed encoding has shown that high-value items are often remembered better than low-value items. Pupillometry revealed that larger dilations for high-value items occurred only when value was stable across consecutive trials. When value switched from one trial to the next, the differentiation disappeared, indicating that pupil responses reflect not just effort but also the stability of motivational context

Functional Significance of Pupil Dilation

A synthesis of pupillometry research highlights that dilation serves multiple functions depending on task context. During encoding, dilation reflects voluntary cognitive effort directed toward valuable or salient information. During retrieval, dilation is more closely tied to novelty detection, familiarity, and memory strength. This framework reconciles inconsistencies across prior studies and underscores the utility of pupillometry for distinguishing strategic from automatic memory processes

Learned Memory Biases

False positive feedback (FPF) was used to investigate whether recognition biases can be trained. Findings showed that feedback-induced biases were specific to item features rather than generalizable across domains. Participants were largely unaware of these changes, suggesting that reinforcement histories can subtly and unconsciously shape recognition decision-making

Do Losses Loom Larger than Gains?

Experiments testing whether potential losses are encoded more strongly than equivalent gains challenged predictions from loss aversion theory. Across studies, gains were remembered as well or better than losses, particularly under conditions of limited encoding time. These results indicate that motivational influences on memory do not map directly onto classic findings in value-based decision-making

Retrieval Incentives and Recognition Memory

Recognition memory was tested under conditions where incentives were delivered at retrieval rather than encoding. Point-based rewards for correct recognition and penalties for errors were used to examine whether motivational signals at test influence memory performance. Results suggest that retrieval incentives shift confidence and response bias more than sensitivity, pointing to motivational modulation of evidence thresholds and decision strategies rather than pure memory strength

Prosody and Recognition Accuracy

Analyses of spoken recognition responses revealed that prosodic features such as pitch, loudness, and timing predict accuracy beyond subjective confidence reports. Prosody thus functions as an implicit cue to recognition reliability, reflecting underlying cognitive processes linked to certainty in memory judgments. This work expands understanding of metacognition and has potential applications in forensic and communication contexts
